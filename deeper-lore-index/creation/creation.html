Ashes of Divinity

The World Unfolded

In the beginning, there was only the Veil—a shapeless ocean of raw potential, timeless and infinite. From this eternal nothingness stirred a thought, and that thought became a voice: Auralis, the Prime Creator. Neither god nor mortal, Auralis was the first will to rise from the Veil, a force of creation unshackled by form or intent. With a breath, Auralis spoke the first word—a name so profound it tore the Veil asunder, and from it sprang the Realms.

Seven threads of reality emerged, woven into the fabric of existence: flame, stone, air, sea, light, shadow, and the unseen thread—time. These would become the Pillars of Creation, each one birthing a world shaped in its image. With each thread came a guardian, a divine being molded from Auralis’ voice. These beings would later be named gods, though they were little more than fragments of the Prime Will.

Auralis crafted the Celestial Realms first, sculpting stars into sentinels and realms into sanctuaries. Planes emerged, layered like pages in an unending book: the Material Realm, cradled between Light and Shadow; the Elemental Boundaries, where flame kissed sea and stone embraced sky; and beyond them, the Astral Tides, a drifting sea of dreams and forgotten thoughts.

Among these divine creations, Auralis wove a final secret: a sliver of the Veil, hidden deep within the folds of reality. It was from this sliver that Riftborn would one day emerge, tethered not to any god, but to the unknowable will of the world itself.

But Auralis did not act alone forever. Other wills awakened in the echo of that first word. Some were born of curiosity, others of opposition. From these came the first gods of rivalry and balance, each claiming dominion over realms and laws. Conflict was inevitable, but so too was beauty, and from this divine tension, the world flourished.

And so, from silence came song, from stillness came motion, and from the unformed Veil came all things.

This was the First Light—the dawn of the gods, the birth of the world, and the first page in a story that has never truly ended.

In the age after the First Light, Auralis withdrew from creation, content to watch the weaving of reality from afar. In the Prime Creator’s absence, seven gods rose to prominence, each born of a thread of the original Veil, and each crowned with divine purpose. These were the gods who came to shape the known world, their names whispered in temples and etched into stone.

Solenar, Flame of Dawn – God of Light and Renewal. Radiant and bold, Solenar is the patron of the rising sun and the unyielding spirit of hope. He brings life to the land with his touch and banishes shadow with every breath. His light fuels crops, burns corruption, and marks every new beginning.

Caldrith, The Forged Hand – God of Stone and Creation. Patron of artisans, inventors, and builders, Caldrith formed the bones of the world. His hammer rang across the mountains, shaping the foundations of dwarven halls and elven towers alike. He governs invention, tradition, and the spark of creation born from discipline.

Elowara, Breath of Storms – Goddess of Air and Guidance. The ever-wandering wind, Elowara governs freedom, travel, and unseen pathways. She carries the prayers of the lost and whispers inspiration into the minds of dreamers. Her wings stir the clouds and her laughter rides the storm.

Thaless, Womb of the Deep – Goddess of Sea and Change. Deep and unknowable, Thaless is mother to the tides and the patron of sailors, secrets, and rebirth. She is kindness and wrath, calm and flood, the embodiment of nature’s shifting will.

Lysera, The Dawnsinger – Goddess of Healing and the Eternal Cycle. A being of luminous serenity, Lysera governs birth, growth, and the balance of life and death. Her followers tend the sick, guide souls, and honor the passing of seasons. Her divine song is said to resonate in every act of compassion.

Vaereth, Shadow of Knowing – God of Secrets and Memory. A quiet and elusive deity, Vaereth is the keeper of truths best forgotten and memories too precious to fade. He walks the space between things—between life and death, truth and lie, past and future.

Chronas, The Silent Wheel – Deity of Time and Fate. Neither god nor goddess, Chronas is a cosmic constant. The passage of years, the twist of fate, the hourglass in every life. Unmoving, unchanging, yet ever present, Chronas weaves the destinies of mortals and gods alike.

These seven, known collectively as the Celestial Accord, established balance and law across the realms. Their influence touches every aspect of mortal life, their temples standing as monuments to divine purpose.

Vyrndal, The Rooted Sky – God of Nature and Arcane Balance. Born from the seed of Vaereth and the light of Lysera, Vyrndal took root at the center of the Ironwood. He is the bridge between arcane knowledge and the natural world, the source from which druids draw their strength and elven scholars seek understanding. His breath is the wind that rustles leaves in forgotten groves, and his eyes blink from the stars when mages look upward for answers.

The gods of the Accord did not only choose those who would build temples or wage wars. They named protectors of story, memory, and beauty—so that the world might flourish in more than just survival.

These gods, shaped from divine fragments or summoned through trial and divine rite, make up the living pantheon of the present age. They are worshipped in cities and remote villages alike, their names carved into sacred stones and whispered in birth rites and funeral songs. Each represents a facet of the mortal experience: from chaos to clarity, destruction to rebirth, endings to legacy.

Lyriviel, The Laughing Mist – Goddess of Trickery and Freedom. From the song of Elowara and a stolen whisper of Vaereth, Lyriviel danced into being during a time when law began to outpace joy. She is not worshipped so much as welcomed—by thieves who pray to avoid notice, by jesters who seek truth through humor, and by the desperate who risk everything for one moment of luck. Her presence is the coin flipped on a whim, the clever word that saves a life, the mist that hides the path not yet taken.

Thrynis, The Wild Tempest – God of Chaos and Destruction. Where Elowara and Thaless once clashed in divine discord, the fury birthed Thrynis—a living tempest with no anchor but his own will. He is a god of raw, unshaped power: the volcano before it erupts, the storm before it strikes. Sailors curse him, yet pray to him. Warriors dream of his blessing, yet fear his gaze. Thrynis does not demand worship; he commands survival.

Drathar, The Warlord Eternal – God of War and Conquest. Forged in the crucible of the War of Chains, Drathar was chosen by Solenar and Caldrith as the answer to a world drowning in directionless bloodshed. Clad in ever-burning armor, Drathar seeks not endless violence, but worthy conflict. His code is honored by generals who fight with strategy, by soldiers who defend their kin, and by those who raise arms only when no words remain.

Valgros, The Stone Titan – God of Strength and Defense. Chiseled from the bones of the world by Caldrith’s mighty forge and given life by Lysera’s gentle breath, Valgros stands as a sentinel in the heart of every mountain. He speaks in stone and silence. Dwarves invoke him when laying foundations; Goliaths chant his name before they clash in ritual battle. He is not merely strong—he is steadfast, a god of shields and shelters, of holding the line no matter the storm.

Kalthor, The Whispering Shade – God of Death and Judgment. When the first mortal passed beyond the veil, Chronas split shadow from soul, and from it shaped Kalthor. Neither cruel nor kind, Kalthor watches the balance of life and death with unflinching patience. He guides the dying gently, judges the lost with silence, and offers no comfort but certainty. His lantern is lit at every grave, and his voice is heard in the stillness between breaths.

Zinvaris, The Veilkeeper – Goddess of Secrets and Loss. In the wake of Kalthor’s creation, a single sorrow refused to fade. From that grief—Lysera’s regret and Vaereth’s knowledge of what must be hidden—came Zinvaris. She is the goddess of mourning and memory, cloaked in the forgotten. Shadar-Kai call her their first mother, and oracles dream of her veil. She does not answer prayers—she accepts them. All that is lost finds a place in her silence.

Lysera, The Dawn’s Embrace – Goddess of Light and Renewal. Once the radiant heart of the Accord, Lysera was reforged in the age after the Twilight Isles’ creation. When Auralis, in an unprecedented act, bestowed her the right to create life, the forging of the Kalashtar and Shadar-Kai changed her. In creating life alone, she passed through divine fire and emerged anew—as the Dawn’s Embrace. She is now worshipped as the balm of morning after night’s end, the warm breath that brings clarity and healing. Her tears became the morning dew, and her hands cradle all those seeking new beginnings.

Thalyn, The Eternal Hearth – Goddess of Community and Harvest. From the still warmth between Thaless’ tides and Caldrith’s embers, Thalyn rose with open arms and calloused hands. She tends the crops, stirs the pot, and blesses every home built with love. She is the flickering candle in a cold room, the shared meal after mourning, the hand that wipes away tears without asking why.

Eryndra, The Tides Eternal – Goddess of Water and Change. Though Thaless governed the sea in its purest form, the shifting tides of the world birthed Eryndra, a younger goddess shaped by change itself. Her voice is in the crash of waves, her heart in the tides that shape continents. In the Empire of Tides, her name is sung with reverence, for she brings both flood and abundance.

Though their origins lie in the Accord, these gods are not lesser for their mortal sparks. It is their understanding of struggle, of imperfection, that grants them a closeness to the world that even the original seven never knew. In this, many say, they are the true gods of mortals.

It is said that when Auralis faded into silence, it was not abandonment, but trust—that the world would continue not with command, but with choice. These gods are that choice. Each one is proof that even the smallest flicker of will can spark the divine.

And so the tapestry continues to be woven: thread by thread, god by god, belief by belief. The pantheon grows not with conquest, but with the echo of prayers, the weight of dreams, and the stories passed from one generation to the next.

These are the gods who walk with us, who shape the storms and cradle the hearthfires. These are the divine legacies of the Accord.

And though some truths have been lost to shadow and silence, the world turns still, watched by gods known and unknown, forgotten and reborn.

For now, their names remain sacred.

Their stories, eternal.

When the divine pantheon had been named and the Accord fulfilled, the gods turned their focus toward the world itself—a realm brimming with potential but still untouched by hands of true creation. It was agreed that from the earth, sea, and sky would rise the Firstborn, mortal children shaped not merely of soil and spirit, but of purpose. These were not accidents of nature. They were gifts, crafted and placed with intention.

The Dwarves were first. Carved from the molten blood of the mountains and hammered into life on the anvils of Auralis himself, they were gifted with the sacred craft of shaping Ironwood. This wood, neither fully of the earth nor the arcane, was a living fusion of material and magic—unknowable to all others. The dwarves alone could shape it, could feel the rhythm of its grain and understand its secrets. It is said that even gods cannot replicate the dwarves' artistry with Ironwood, no matter how many centuries pass. This gift was not just craftsmanship. It was a trust—that the world would always have builders.

The Elves were born from the twilight between Vyrndal and Lysera, given form among the trees of the Ironwood and the shores of the Tides Eternal. Their gift was magic, woven into their very blood, and foresight, to guide their long lives with wisdom. They were the first to speak the True Tongue of spellcraft, and their vision often stretched beyond the horizon, glimpsing fate as others saw only fog. Their role was that of guides and keepers—lore-bearers to remind the world of what had been and what must never be forgotten.

The Humans came next, shaped from bare clay and kindled with ambition. Their gift was adaptability: the power to craft wonders from nothing but stone, sand, and air. Where dwarves required sacred tools, and elves required ancient magic, humans needed only the will to try. It was this spark—this refusal to be bound by limits—that made them so dangerous, and so beloved. Their creativity gave rise to kingdoms, to chaos, and to legacy.

The Halflings were not formed in the image of ambition, but in the spirit of harmony. Thalyn, the Eternal Hearth, shaped them with laughter in her heart and Eryndra placed them in a land untouched by war: Eltoria, the Living Wilds. Their gift was unity with nature, a life lived in rhythm with the world rather than in defiance of it. They were not mighty warriors or ambitious kings, but caretakers. And in their quiet joy, they preserved balance long forgotten by many others.

The Kalashtar and Shadar-Kai were unlike the others. They were born not of stone or storm, but of a pact between Auralis and Lysera. The only god permitted to craft true life, Lysera was entrusted with a sacred purpose: to create beings that would uphold the balance between dawn and twilight, between hope and loss. In the age of light, the Kalashtar flourished, luminous in thought and spirit. In the era of dusk, the Shadar-Kai rose, solemn and shadowed, keepers of secrets and grief. Neither is whole without the other, and each turn of the celestial cycle reminds the world that balance is not peace—it is vigilance.

The Goliaths were shaped in the aftermath of storms, where Thrynis' chaos had scoured the land bare and Valgros' stone had stood unbroken. They are will given flesh: towering, resolute, defiant. Their gift was strength unmatched, but not merely of arm or bone—they were born to endure. To carry what others could not. To hold the mountain when it crumbled. To rage not from hatred, but from burden.

The Aasimar were not made. They were chosen. When Lysera wept at the first mortal death, her tears fell into the dreams of sleeping children across the world. Some awoke changed—their souls bearing the echo of divine purpose. These were the Aasimar, gifted with radiant clarity, and the uncanny ability to master a craft, calling, or creed in years where others required lifetimes. They are the answers to prayers not yet spoken.

The Tabaxi were the last of the Firstborn, and perhaps the most enigmatic. Vyrndal shaped them from the wildest parts of the world—from the stone peaks and the deepest shadows. They walk between worlds: silent as ghosts, swift as lightning. Their gift was a trinity—to move with the earth, to see as beasts see, and to vanish where no path should exist. They are hunters, poets, tricksters, and prophets. In every Tabaxi stirs the ancient pulse of something older than war or song: instinct.

Thus were the Firstborn made, not to rule, but to shape the world through their gifts.

And though more would come in later ages, born of mingled blood and broken oaths, it was these first children of the gods who carried the dreams of creation into the waking world.

They are the seeds of every story.
The bones beneath every stone.
The breath before the name.

And so the world began to live.
