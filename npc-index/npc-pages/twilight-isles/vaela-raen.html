Vaela Raen – The Moon’s Devotion


Vaela Raen was born as a Dawn Cycle faded.

Not at the end of all dawns—just the closing of her own. She was still a child when the skies began to change, when the golden light that had always touched the sea dimmed, and the first hints of eternal twilight crept into the corners of the day.

And then came the night.

Not just any night, but the celestial turning—a moment that only the Twilight Isles truly know. An eclipse veiled the sun, and the blood moon emerged, full and forever. The sky above her shimmered and twisted, falling into itself with slow, graceful urgency. Starfalls streaked the heavens in swirls of silver and violet, like the gods were brushing the sky clean with stardust.

Vaela stood beneath that sky and forgot to breathe.

Others marveled. Others feared.

Vaela fell in love.

She did not understand it fully. She was just a child, after all. But something in her bones remembered. Something ancient stirred in the marrow of her soul and whispered, You were made for this.

From that night forward, she followed the moon.

She charted its path along the temple walls. She memorized its cycles. She prayed not to the blazing dawn, but to the quiet glow above the still water. When others recited the songs of morning, Vaela hummed lullabies to the stars.

She became a priestess of Lysera, but not of the sun.

Of the night.

Her faith was no less fierce. Just gentler. Wilder. Where sun-priests shouted their devotion into the skies, Vaela whispered hers into moonlit pools. Her rituals honored reflection, surrender, transformation. She mapped the constellations across cycles and traced forgotten starpaths in chalk and prayer.

Her voice is soft, but never weak. She speaks like the tide—steady, measured, impossible to ignore if you know how to listen. Her eyes, pale as sea-glass, seem to hold entire night skies within them.

Vaela’s magic is woven from dreams and starlight. She can soothe nightmares into stories. Call gentle light into the darkest spaces. She does not burn. She glows.

And in a world where dusk has become dominion, she remains one of the few who sees beauty in its silence.

Of Silver Eyes and a Sky That Called Her


Zostara never planned to leave.

Not really.

She talked about it, of course—like a storm talks about breaking. Like a dreamer talks about cities she’s never seen. The world beyond the Isles was a myth, a painting glimpsed in pieces through songs and secondhand maps. She asked about it sometimes in class, bold and restless and too curious for her own good. But curiosity, in the Twilight Isles, was often mistaken for arrogance.

The temple called her gifted. Her tutors called her intense.

Vaela Raen called her bright.

They first spoke under moonlight—not during class, not in ceremony, but in the temple’s overgrown garden, long after curfew. Zostara had slipped outside to escape a lecture she couldn’t sit through. Vaela had simply… been there. As she often was. Sitting on the low wall with a cup of tea and a book that glowed faintly under the stars.

“You run like the sun’s still chasing you,” she said, not looking up.

Zostara froze. “Sorry. I didn’t mean to—”

“I didn’t say leave.”

She hesitated, then sat beside her.

For a while, they said nothing. The night was thick with songspinners and silvermoths. The moon hung red and full above the canopy, a perfect mirror of Vaela’s quiet gaze.

Eventually, Zostara asked, “Did you ever want to leave?”

Vaela turned a page. “Once.”

“And?”

“I stayed. But only because the thing I was chasing came here instead.”

Zostara didn’t understand. Not fully. Not then.

But it stayed with her.

Over the weeks that followed, she began finding excuses to speak with Vaela. Not just in temple halls, but in the garden, the archives, the quiet places where others didn’t tread. They talked about dreams. About magic. About worship and contradiction. About how some prayers are sung loud, and others whispered so softly you only notice they worked after you’ve forgotten you spoke them.

Vaela never told her to stay.

She never told her to go.

She simply listened, and in doing so, she built a bridge that no other priest or mentor ever had.

When the decision finally came—when Zostara stood at the edge of the temple path, bag slung over her shoulder and heart pounding like a storm trapped in glass—it was Vaela who found her there.

“You don’t need anyone’s permission,” she said.

“I don’t know what I’m looking for,” Zostara whispered.

Vaela brushed a silver charm into her hand. A smooth moonstone wrapped in a strand of starlace.

“Good,” she said. “It means you’ll know it when you see it.”

Zostara wept.

Not with sorrow. But with the terrifying freedom of a future unlabeled.

They embraced only once. It was enough. The kind of embrace that marks a moment in time, so when the years stretch and the path winds, you can trace yourself back to it and say There. That’s where I chose.

Now, whenever Zostara looks up and sees the moon hanging over a strange horizon—unfamiliar stars, unknown skies—she thinks of soft eyes, silver words, and the woman who never told her who to be.

Only that she could be anything.
